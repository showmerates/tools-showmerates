The Honest Math On Waiting

Most "cost of waiting" calculators are sales tactics dressed up as analysis. They assume aggressive home price growth, no rate drops, and ignore your rent. Of course the answer comes out "buy now!" But the real math is more nuanced — sometimes waiting genuinely is the right move. This calculator runs both scenarios honestly so you can decide for yourself.

The Four Variables That Actually Matter

1. What home prices do during the wait. If prices rise, you pay more for the same house later. If prices fall (as in some 2025-2026 markets), you save. As of early 2026, the Case-Shiller index shows annual home price appreciation around 1-2% nationally — far below the 5-8% of the recent boom. Tampa, Phoenix, and other previously hot markets are actually declining. Northeast and Midwest are still appreciating modestly.

2. What rates do during the wait. If rates drop, your monthly payment is lower on the same purchase — or you can refinance later if you buy now (a VA IRRRL streamline refi makes this easy and cheap). If rates rise, your monthly payment is higher. Nobody can reliably predict this; ignore anyone who claims they can.

3. The rent you pay during the wait. This is the silent killer most calculators ignore. If you wait 12 months at $2,200/mo, you're paying $26,400 to your landlord with zero return. That's the largest single number in the calculation for most people.

4. The equity you'd build by buying now. Every monthly payment on your own mortgage builds principal in your name. Even modest principal paydown over a year ($4,000-$6,000 on a typical loan) plus any appreciation flows to your net worth instead of your landlord's.

The VA-Specific Reframe

VA borrowers have an advantage that conventional borrowers don't: the IRRRL. If you buy today at 6.5% and rates drop to 5.5% in 18 months, you can refinance into the lower rate via VA Streamline with minimal closing costs and no appraisal. So the downside of "wrong rate timing" is much smaller for Veterans than for conventional buyers. You're not locked into your purchase rate for 30 years — you can refresh it almost any time rates make sense.

This single fact tilts most "should I wait?" calculations in favor of buying now for VA-eligible buyers, because the rate risk is largely hedged by your refinance option. The home price risk and rent cost remain, but they're already favoring "buy now" in most scenarios.

When Waiting Actually Makes Sense

Wait if any of these are true:

  • You're not financially ready (no reserves, unstable income, unresolved credit issues)
  • You're in a market with clearly declining prices and no rebound signs
  • You're planning to PCS or move within 2-3 years
  • Rates have spiked recently and you have strong reason to expect normalization soon
  • Rent in your area is genuinely cheap relative to ownership (rare but exists)

Buy if any of these are true:

  • You're financially stable and planning to stay 5+ years
  • Rent in your area exceeds the carrying cost of ownership
  • Your local market is appreciating or flat (most markets in 2026)
  • You have VA eligibility and can use $0 down to your advantage
  • Your goal is wealth-building rather than rate optimization

What This Calculator Doesn't Include

To keep the math focused, we don't model: tax deductibility of mortgage interest (which favors buying), maintenance costs and homeowners insurance during ownership (which favor renting), the opportunity cost of your down payment in alternative investments, or the psychological value of owning your own space. These all matter for the full picture but vary too much by individual to standardize.

We also don't model market timing risk in either direction. The future isn't predictable, which is why this calculator lets you adjust every assumption. The smartest way to use it is to run the scenarios you most fear (rates spike, prices crash, you have to move early) and see whether the decision holds up.

Real Talk From an LO

The truth most mortgage marketing won't tell you: in normal markets with normal appreciation, the cost of waiting is usually positive but rarely catastrophic. The bigger risk to your financial life isn't getting the timing wrong — it's spending five years of indecision while your rent goes up, your savings stagnate, and your housing wealth sits at zero. Veterans who buy when they're personally ready and stay put for the long haul almost always come out ahead of those who try to time the market perfectly. Use this calculator to confirm or challenge your gut, not to manufacture urgency.
