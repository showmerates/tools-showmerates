Discount Points, In Plain English

Buying discount points means paying cash upfront to permanently lower your interest rate. One point equals 1% of your loan amount and typically reduces your rate by about 0.25%. It's prepaid interest — you give the lender money today to pay less interest every month for the rest of the loan.

The Single Calculation That Matters

Break-even period equals the cost of the points divided by your monthly payment savings. If your points cost $8,000 and you save $129/month, you break even after about 62 months (5.2 years). Stay longer than break-even and you come out ahead. Sell or refinance before break-even and you lost money on the points.

Why Most Buyers Get This Wrong

Two common mistakes:

Mistake 1: Overstating their timeline. Buyers say "I'll be here 10 years" but the average homeowner moves every 8 years, and many refinance within 3–5 years if rates drop. Be honest with yourself.

Mistake 2: Ignoring refinance risk. If you buy 2 points to drop your rate from 6.5% to 6.0%, then rates fall to 5.0% in two years and you refinance — you just paid $8,000 for a benefit you barely used. The 2-year savings would only be about $3,100. You lose roughly $4,900 on the points.

When Points Make Sense (And When They Don't)

Buy points if:

  • You plan to stay 7+ years
  • You're confident you won't refinance (rates already low, or stable)
  • You have cash beyond your emergency fund
  • You've maxed out other tax-advantaged options
  • The break-even is within your realistic timeline

Skip points if:

  • You might move in less than 5 years
  • Rates are unusually high and likely to drop
  • You'd rather put cash into your home equity (larger down payment)
  • The break-even is past year 8
  • You're tight on closing-day cash

Tax Treatment (Worth Knowing)

Discount points paid on a purchase loan are fully tax-deductible in the year you pay them, per IRS Publication 936. On a $400,000 loan, paying 2 points = $8,000 deduction. At a 22% marginal tax bracket, that's about $1,760 back at tax time — reducing your effective point cost. This calculator doesn't factor in the tax benefit, so your real break-even might be slightly shorter than what's shown.

Note: points on a refinance are deductible too, but spread over the life of the loan rather than all in year one. And consult a tax professional — this isn't tax advice.

A Better Alternative Sometimes: Larger Down Payment

If you have $8,000 in cash and want to use it productively, paying down principal instead of buying points can sometimes do more work. A larger down payment reduces both the loan amount AND your monthly payment, and gives you instant equity rather than a future-dated benefit. For VA buyers specifically, this is more nuanced because $0 down is a feature — sometimes the right move is to skip both the points and the down payment, and keep the cash invested elsewhere.

Real Talk From an LO

The honest answer most lenders won't tell you: in a rate environment where rates are likely to drop within 2–3 years, buying down your rate is usually a losing bet. You're paying for a 30-year benefit that you'll cancel via refinance after 24 months. The math only works if the savings between now and refinance exceed the cost of the points — which they rarely do at 0.25% rate reductions. Run the scenarios honestly and ask your LO for a no-points quote alongside any buydown offer. If they can only sell you the buydown, you're talking to the wrong LO.
