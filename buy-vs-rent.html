The Buy vs Rent Question, Done Right

Most online buy-vs-rent calculators were built for civilian buyers with conventional or FHA loans. They assume you need 20% down, build in PMI costs, and ignore the unique advantages of a VA loan. For Veterans, the math is significantly different — usually in your favor.

What Makes the VA Math Different

Three things change the calculation completely for VA-eligible buyers:

1. $0 Down Payment

You don't need to save up a 20% down payment. That's $70,000 on a $350,000 home that stays in your pocket — available for emergency reserves, investments, or simply not waiting another five years to buy.

2. No Private Mortgage Insurance (PMI)

Conventional and FHA loans charge $150–$400/month in PMI when you put less than 20% down. VA loans don't — ever. That's $1,800–$4,800 per year that stays in your pocket and compounds for the entire life of the loan.

3. Generally Lower Rates

VA loans usually carry interest rates a quarter to a half percent below comparable conventional loans. Combined with the no-PMI savings, your effective monthly cost is often significantly lower than what civilian calculators assume.

How This Calculator Works

The calculator runs both scenarios year-by-year:

Buying: Each year you build equity through principal paydown and home appreciation. You pay property tax, insurance, and maintenance. At your exit year, the calculator subtracts your remaining loan balance and selling costs (typically 6–8% of sale price) to find your net wealth.

Renting + Investing: Each year you pay rent (which increases at the rate you set), plus renters insurance. The money you would have spent on owning costs — closing costs, the difference between rent and a higher monthly housing cost — gets invested at your chosen return rate. Final wealth is what that invested money has grown to.

The breakeven year is when buying first overtakes renting. The longer you stay past breakeven, the more buying typically wins — because rent inflation never stops, while your principal-and-interest payment stays fixed.

The Hidden Factor Civilian Calculators Miss

Rent inflation. Most calculators let you set rent inflation, but they default to 1–2%. The reality is closer to 3–4% over long periods, and during pandemic years it was 8%+. Every year your rent goes up. Every year your mortgage principal-and-interest stays exactly the same. After 10 years of even modest rent inflation, your monthly rent is 30–40% higher than when you started — while your mortgage payment is identical to day one. This is the single biggest argument for buying when you can.

When Renting Actually Wins

To be fair, renting can win in specific situations:

Short time horizons. If you're moving in less than 3–4 years, the closing costs and selling costs eat up your gains. Buying is rarely the right move for short-term housing.

Hot, then cold markets. If you buy at a peak and sell during a correction, you can lose money. Real estate is generally a long game.

Maintenance-heavy properties. Older homes can cost 2–3% of value per year in upkeep. The calculator defaults to 1%, which is reasonable for newer homes but conservative for older ones.

Strong stock market years. If you'd invest aggressively and your money compounds at 10%+, renting can occasionally outperform — though that assumes you actually invest the difference, which most renters don't.

Real Talk From Terry

I've worked with hundreds of Veterans deciding whether to use their VA benefit. The pattern is consistent: those who buy with VA in their first 1–2 years of eligibility almost always come out ahead by year 5, regardless of market timing. Those who wait "until rates are lower" or "until they save more" usually pay more in cumulative rent than they would have saved on a lower rate. The VA benefit is one of the most powerful wealth-building tools in the federal government. If you can comfortably afford the monthly payment and plan to stay 4+ years, the math almost always says: use it.
