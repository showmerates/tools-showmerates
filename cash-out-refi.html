How VA Cash-Out Refinance Math Actually Works

A cash-out refinance replaces your existing mortgage with a new, larger VA loan and you pocket the difference. On its own that doesn't save you money — it just shifts debt around. But when you combine it with paying off high-interest debt AND lowering your mortgage rate, the math can be genuinely powerful. The key word is can. Most calculators oversimplify this; here's the honest version.

The Three Real Sources of Savings

1. Lower mortgage rate. If your current rate is meaningfully higher than what's available today (say, 1 percentage point or more), refinancing the existing balance saves you money every month for the life of the loan. This is the obvious one.

2. Debt consolidation. This is where the real magic usually happens. Credit cards at 22% APR, personal loans at 11%, HELOCs at variable rates — paying these off and rolling them into a 6% mortgage can drop your effective monthly debt service by hundreds or thousands of dollars. Even when total interest paid over the life of the loan is higher (mortgages amortize over 30 years), monthly cash flow improves dramatically.

3. Resetting amortization on existing high-rate principal. If you're 7 years into a 30-year loan at 7.25%, you've been paying a lot of interest and very little principal. Refinancing into a fresh 30-year at a lower rate dramatically reduces your monthly principal-and-interest payment — but it also extends how long you'll be paying. This calculator focuses on monthly cash flow, not long-term total interest, because cash flow is what people actually feel.

What the Closing Cash Looks Like (The Honest Version)

This is where lender marketing gets sloppy. Here's what actually happens at a refinance closing:

What comes out of your equity: Closing costs (typically $4,000-$8,000 on a VA refi), the VA funding fee (2.15% first use, 3.30% subsequent use, unless you're exempt), payoff of your current mortgage balance, payoff of any consolidated debts. All of this typically gets rolled into the new loan amount.

What comes back to you: The remaining "cash out" amount goes to your bank account a few days after closing. You'll also receive an escrow refund check from your old lender within 15-30 days — this is the unused balance that was sitting in your escrow account for property taxes and insurance.

The "skipped payment" trick: You typically don't make your first new mortgage payment for 30-60 days after closing. This feels like free money but it isn't — you're prepaying daily interest at closing for the rest of that month, and your first payment covers the following month's interest. It's still a real cash flow benefit because you get to skip writing the check, but it's not actually new savings.

The Lender Myths You'll Hear

"Skip 2 months of payments!" — This is largely marketing. You're paying daily interest at closing and your first payment is timed normally. You might genuinely skip one payment cycle depending on closing date, but it's not "free money."

"Get your escrow refund as bonus cash!" — That's your own money the prior servicer was holding. You'd have gotten it anyway eventually.

"No money out of pocket!" — True in the sense that closing costs and funding fee get financed into the loan. But you're paying interest on those costs for 30 years if you don't pay them down.

When Cash-Out Makes Sense

The cleanest scenarios are: you have meaningful high-interest debt (credit cards, personal loans), enough home equity to cover the cash-out without going above 90% loan-to-value, and a current mortgage rate that's at least 0.75–1.0% above market. If all three are true, the math usually works overwhelmingly in your favor.

It also makes sense for home improvements at a much lower rate than a HELOC, paying for college without taking on student loans, or building an emergency fund if you're truly capital-constrained — but those are individual decisions, not pure savings plays.

When It Doesn't Make Sense

If your current rate is already low (5% or below), cash-out usually doesn't save monthly — your new payment after factoring in closing costs and funding fee will be higher even with a lower rate. If you have no high-interest debt to consolidate, the only benefit is the cash itself, and you're paying real closing costs to get money you could probably borrow more cheaply elsewhere (HELOC, 0% APR card balance transfer, etc.) for short-term needs.

If you plan to sell within 2-3 years, you may not recoup the closing costs through monthly savings.

The VA-Specific Advantages

VA cash-out has a few real edges over conventional cash-out: no PMI ever (saves $100-300/month vs conventional), more lenient credit/DTI underwriting, and the funding fee (which is higher than a purchase but financed into the loan). Many lenders cap conventional cash-out at 80% LTV, while VA programs technically allow up to 100% (most lenders impose 90% overlays in 2026 but we are able to finance up to 100% in most cases). That higher allowable LTV means more cash available, more debts payable, more potential consolidation savings.

If you're exempt from the funding fee due to a service-connected disability, your VA cash-out beats conventional cash-out by a wide margin — you skip the largest single cost.

Real Talk From an LO

Cash-out refinance is one of the most useful tools in mortgage when the math works, and one of the most over-pitched products when it doesn't. The honest test: if I removed the closing costs and funding fee from your scenario entirely, would the new loan still be worth it on monthly savings alone? If yes, the refi is genuinely solid. If no, you're paying real money for marginal benefit. Ask your LO to run both numbers and don't accept marketing language that obscures the real cost.
