The VA MAP Rule, In Plain English

The VA Interest Rate Reduction Refinance Loan (IRRRL) is one of the simplest refinances in the mortgage industry — but only if it passes the VA's MAP Rule (38 U.S.C. § 3709). The MAP Rule is what stops lenders from "churning" Veterans into bad refinances. Here's what your loan has to clear.

The 36-Month Recoup Test

The single most important rule. Your recoupable closing costs, divided by your monthly principal-and-interest savings, must equal 36 months or less. If it takes longer than 36 months to break even, the VA will not guarantee the loan — meaning most lenders won't approve it.

Recoupment (months) = Recoupable Closing Costs ÷ Monthly P&I Savings
If the result is ≤ 36 months, the loan passes. If it's > 36 months, it fails and is ineligible.

What Counts (and What Doesn't) in the Recoup Calc

This is where most online IRRRL calculators get it wrong. The VA explicitly excludes certain costs from the recoupment calculation:

  • Excluded: VA funding fee (paid under chapter 37)
  • Excluded: Property taxes (current and prepaid)
  • Excluded: Homeowners insurance (prepaid)
  • Excluded: Amounts held in escrow at closing
  • Excluded: HOA fees, special assessments
  • Included: Lender fees, origination charges, points
  • Included: Title services, recording fees, attorney fees
  • Included: Appraisal (if required for your loan type)

Other IRRRL Requirements You Need to Pass

The 36-month recoup is the big one, but it's not the only test. To qualify for an IRRRL, you must also meet these criteria:

  • Seasoning — 210 days: At least 210 days must have passed since the first payment due date on your current VA loan.
  • Seasoning — 6 payments: You must have made at least six consecutive monthly payments on your existing loan.
  • Rate reduction (fixed-to-fixed): The new rate must usually be at least 0.50 percentage points lower than your current rate.
  • Existing VA loan required: An IRRRL only refinances an existing VA loan. You can't IRRRL out of a conventional or FHA loan.
  • Net Tangible Benefit: The loan must provide a measurable benefit to you — lower payment, lower rate, or term reduction.

Special Cases the Standard Calculation Doesn't Cover

ARM-to-fixed and term reductions: If you're refinancing from an ARM to a fixed rate or shortening your loan term, the rate reduction rule may not apply — but the 36-month recoup still does.

Payment-stays-the-same scenarios: If your new P&I payment is the same or higher (rare), you can still qualify only if you incur no fees or closing costs other than taxes, escrow, and the funding fee.

Energy Efficient Mortgages (EEM): Costs added for EEM improvements are excluded from the recoup calc since they "pay for themselves" through energy savings.

Real Talk From Terry

I've seen Veterans get sold on IRRRLs that "barely pass" recoup at 35 months. Technically eligible, but practically a bad deal — you're paying $6,000 in costs to save $170 a month. If you're going to refinance, aim for a recoup period under 24 months. That's when an IRRRL really starts saving you real money. Anything over 30 months, ask yourself if it's worth tying up capital that long.
